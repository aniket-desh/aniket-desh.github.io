An Analytic Side Tangent

01.09.2024

This short post presents a classical result in real analysis: one that I encountered early on in Rudin’s Principles of Mathematical Analysis and managed to prove independently. The statement concerns the monotonicity of a sequence that converges to the exponential function. While the proof technique here is elementary and admittedly a bit roundabout, it demonstrates how combinatorics and inequalities interact to reveal deep properties of limits and convergence.

Consider the sequence \( a_n = \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n \), where \( x > 0 \). This expression famously converges to \( e^x \), but our focus here is on showing that the sequence is strictly increasing. This is a foundational property in analysis and a compelling showcase of how structure emerges from repeated products.

Let’s begin with the precise statement.

theorem.
Let \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) be positive. Then the sequence \( \left\{ \left(1 + \frac{x}{n} \right)^n \right\}_{n=1}^{\infty} \) is strictly increasing.

We now present the proof.

proof.
We begin by recalling the binomial theorem: \[ (a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k \] Applying this to our sequence, we expand the \(n\)th term: \[ \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} \left(1\right)^{n-k} \left(\frac{x}{n}\right)^k = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} \frac{x^k}{n^k} \] Extracting the \(k = 0\) term gives: \[ \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \cdot \frac{x^k}{n^k} \] We now express the factorial and power terms in product form: \[ n! = \left( \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} (n - i) \right)(n - k)! \quad \text{and} \quad n^k = \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} n \] Substituting, the binomial coefficient simplifies to: \[ \binom{n}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{n^k} = \left( \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n}\right) \right) \cdot \frac{1}{k!} \] Thus, \[ \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{n} \left( \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n}\right) \right) \cdot \frac{x^k}{k!} \] Now consider the \((n+1)\)st term: \[ \left(1 + \frac{x}{n+1}\right)^{n+1} = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{n+1} \left( \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n+1}\right) \right) \cdot \frac{x^k}{k!} \] We separate the last term for clarity: \[ = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{n} \left( \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n+1}\right) \right) \cdot \frac{x^k}{k!} + \left( \prod_{i=0}^{n} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n+1} \right) \right) \cdot \frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \] To show that the sequence is strictly increasing, we compare the coefficients term-by-term: Since \( n < n+1 \), we have: \[ \frac{1}{n} > \frac{1}{n+1} \quad \Rightarrow \quad 1 - \frac{i}{n} < 1 - \frac{i}{n+1} \] Therefore, for each \(k \leq n\), \[ \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n} \right) < \prod_{i=0}^{k-1} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n+1} \right) \] Additionally, the extra term \[ \left( \prod_{i=0}^{n} \left(1 - \frac{i}{n+1} \right) \right) \cdot \frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \] is strictly positive because each factor \( 1 - \frac{i}{n+1} > 0 \) for \( 0 \leq i \leq n \). Hence, every term in the summation at step \(n+1\) is greater than its counterpart at step \(n\), and a new positive term is added. This proves the sequence is strictly increasing. \( \square \)

An alternative and more elegant proof involves taking logarithms and applying the mean value theorem. This approach yields more insight into the convergence of the sequence to \(e^x\) and avoids the combinatorial machinery. Nonetheless, the above proof serves as a nice exercise in inequalities and binomial expansions.
